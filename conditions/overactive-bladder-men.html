Men's Health

Overactive Bladder

Urgency, frequency and nocturia in men — often overlapping with prostate symptoms but requiring a distinct diagnostic and treatment approach.

Overview

What is Overactive Bladder in Men?

Overactive bladder (OAB) is a clinical syndrome defined by the hallmark symptom of urgency — a sudden, compelling need to pass urine that is difficult to defer. It is typically accompanied by urinary frequency (passing urine more than eight times a day), nocturia (waking two or more times at night to urinate), and in some cases urge incontinence (involuntary leakage associated with urgency).

In men, OAB presents a particular diagnostic challenge because its symptoms frequently overlap with — or occur alongside — those caused by benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). The enlarged prostate can itself cause storage symptoms such as urgency and frequency, and longstanding bladder outlet obstruction from BPH can lead to secondary detrusor overactivity, where the bladder muscle becomes overactive as a result of working harder against the obstruction over time.

Regardless of the underlying cause, OAB can significantly affect quality of life, disrupting sleep, limiting social activities, and undermining confidence. The good news is that it is very treatable — but in men, it is essential to first establish whether obstruction from the prostate is contributing, as this fundamentally changes the treatment approach.

OAB in men is different from OAB in women

In women, OAB is usually a primary bladder problem. In men, it can be primary (idiopathic) or secondary to prostatic obstruction. Distinguishing between the two is critical because prescribing bladder-relaxing medication to a man with significant obstruction can worsen symptoms and even cause urinary retention. A proper urodynamic assessment is often essential before starting treatment.

Symptoms

Symptoms of OAB in Men

OAB is primarily a storage disorder — the symptoms relate to how the bladder fills and holds urine, rather than how urine is passed. This is an important distinction from the voiding symptoms (weak stream, hesitancy, straining) that are more typically caused by prostatic obstruction.

OAB Symptoms (Storage)

  • ›
    Urgency — a sudden, compelling need to pass urine that is difficult to postpone (the defining symptom)
  • ›
    Frequency — passing urine more than 8 times during the day
  • ›
    Nocturia — waking 2 or more times at night to urinate, disrupting sleep
  • ›
    Urge incontinence — involuntary leakage of urine accompanied by or immediately preceded by urgency

Voiding Symptoms (Suggest BPH)

  • ›
    Weak or slow stream — reduced force when passing urine
  • ›
    Hesitancy — difficulty initiating urination
  • ›
    Intermittency — the stream starting and stopping
  • ›
    Straining and incomplete emptying — having to push, with a sense the bladder is not fully emptied

Many men have both

It is very common for men to experience a combination of storage and voiding symptoms. When this happens, it is especially important to determine whether the storage symptoms (OAB) are being driven by prostatic obstruction, as this affects which treatments are safe and effective.

Causes

What Causes OAB in Men?

There are several reasons why a man may develop overactive bladder symptoms. In many cases, more than one factor is contributing.

Secondary to BPH / bladder outlet obstruction

This is the most important cause to identify in men. When the prostate obstructs the bladder outlet, the detrusor muscle has to work harder to empty. Over time, this leads to thickening and overactivity of the muscle — the bladder begins contracting involuntarily during filling, producing urgency and frequency. Treating the obstruction often resolves the OAB symptoms.

Idiopathic detrusor overactivity

In some men, the bladder muscle becomes overactive without any identifiable obstruction or neurological cause. This is termed idiopathic OAB and is the same mechanism seen in women with overactive bladder. The detrusor contracts involuntarily during the filling phase, producing the characteristic symptoms of urgency and frequency.

Neurological conditions

Conditions that affect the nervous system can disrupt normal bladder control. These include multiple sclerosis (MS), Parkinson's disease, stroke, spinal cord injury, and diabetic neuropathy. When OAB has a neurological cause, it is referred to as neurogenic detrusor overactivity.

Post-prostatectomy OAB

Some men develop new or worsened OAB symptoms after prostate surgery (radical prostatectomy for cancer, or TURP/HoLEP for BPH). This can be related to changes in the bladder neck, altered nerve supply, or pre-existing detrusor overactivity that was previously masked by obstruction.

Lifestyle and dietary factors

Excessive caffeine and alcohol intake are well-known bladder irritants that can trigger or worsen OAB symptoms. High fluid intake, particularly in the evening, can contribute to nocturia. Certain medications (such as diuretics) can also exacerbate symptoms.

Diagnosis

How is OAB Diagnosed in Men?

Diagnosing OAB in men requires a structured approach to distinguish it from prostatic obstruction and other conditions that can cause similar symptoms. The following investigations are commonly used:

Clinical History

A detailed discussion of your symptoms, their severity, how they affect your daily life, relevant medical history (including neurological conditions), and current medications.

Bladder Diary

A 3-day record of fluid intake, voiding times, volumes passed, and episodes of urgency or leakage. This provides objective evidence of frequency, nocturia, and functional bladder capacity.

Flow Rate & Post-Void Residual

A uroflowmetry test measures the speed and pattern of your urine flow, while an ultrasound scan immediately afterwards checks how much urine is left in the bladder. Together, these help identify whether obstruction is present.

Urodynamics

This is the gold-standard investigation in men with OAB symptoms. It measures bladder pressure during filling and voiding to definitively determine whether the bladder is overactive, whether obstruction is present, or both. This distinction is critical for safe and effective treatment planning.

PSA & Further Tests

A PSA blood test may be performed if prostate enlargement is suspected or to help exclude prostate cancer. Cystoscopy (a camera examination of the bladder) may also be recommended if there are concerns about other bladder pathology.

Treatment

Treatment Options for OAB in Men

Treatment is tailored to the underlying cause identified during assessment. In men, it is essential to address any prostatic obstruction before — or alongside — treating the overactive bladder itself.

1 Conservative Management

Bladder retraining

A structured programme to gradually increase the interval between voids, training the bladder to hold more urine and reducing urgency.

Fluid management

Adjusting the volume and timing of fluid intake — particularly reducing caffeine, alcohol, and evening fluids to improve frequency and nocturia.

Caffeine and alcohol reduction

Both caffeine and alcohol are direct bladder irritants. Reducing or eliminating them can produce a meaningful improvement in urgency and frequency.

Pelvic floor exercises

Strengthening the pelvic floor can help suppress urgency and improve bladder control. This is effective in men as well as women, and particularly useful after prostate surgery.

2 Medical Treatment

Anticholinergics (e.g. solifenacin, tolterodine, oxybutynin)

These medications work by blocking the nerve signals that cause the bladder to contract involuntarily. They are the first-line drug treatment for OAB. Important in men: anticholinergics must be used with caution if there is significant prostatic obstruction, as they can worsen voiding difficulty and, in rare cases, cause urinary retention. Urodynamics should ideally be performed first.

Mirabegron (Betmiga)

A beta-3 adrenoceptor agonist that relaxes the bladder muscle during filling. It has a different side-effect profile to anticholinergics (fewer dry mouth and constipation effects) and may be better tolerated. It can also be used in combination with anticholinergics if a single agent is insufficient.

BPH treatment if obstruction is contributing

If urodynamics confirm that obstruction from the prostate is driving the OAB symptoms, the priority is to relieve the obstruction. This may involve alpha-blockers (e.g. tamsulosin) to relax the prostate, 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (e.g. finasteride) to shrink it, or surgical options such as TURP, HoLEP, or Rezum if medication is insufficient. In many men, resolving the obstruction also resolves the overactive bladder. Read about BPH treatment →

3 Advanced Treatments

If conservative measures and medication do not provide adequate symptom relief, several advanced options are available:

Botox injections into the bladder (onabotulinumtoxinA)

Botulinum toxin is injected directly into the bladder wall via a cystoscope under local anaesthetic. It temporarily paralyses the overactive muscle, reducing involuntary contractions and improving capacity. The effect typically lasts 6-12 months and can be repeated. Men need to be counselled about the small risk of needing temporary self-catheterisation afterwards.

Sacral neuromodulation (SNM)

A small device (similar to a pacemaker) is implanted to deliver gentle electrical impulses to the sacral nerves that control the bladder. This modulates the abnormal nerve signalling responsible for overactivity. A trial stimulation is performed first to confirm benefit before permanent implantation.

Percutaneous tibial nerve stimulation (PTNS)

A non-invasive treatment involving a fine needle placed near the ankle to stimulate the tibial nerve, which shares a common pathway with the nerves controlling the bladder. Sessions are typically weekly for 12 weeks, with maintenance sessions thereafter. It is well-tolerated and requires no anaesthetic.

OAB vs BPH

OAB and BPH — Understanding the Overlap

One of the most important aspects of managing urinary symptoms in men is understanding the relationship between overactive bladder and benign prostatic hyperplasia. These are not mutually exclusive conditions — in fact, they frequently coexist.

BPH causes obstruction of the bladder outlet. The bladder responds by generating higher pressures to overcome this obstruction, which over time causes the detrusor muscle to thicken and become unstable. This secondary detrusor overactivity produces OAB symptoms — urgency, frequency, and nocturia — on top of the voiding symptoms (weak stream, hesitancy) that are directly caused by the prostate.

Why This Matters for Treatment

Risk of wrong treatment

Giving anticholinergics to a man with significant obstruction can worsen voiding and cause retention.

Urodynamics provides the answer

Pressure-flow studies can separate obstruction from pure overactivity, guiding safe treatment.

Treat the right problem first

If obstruction is the driver, relieving it often resolves the OAB. If not, OAB-specific treatment can be safely added.

This is why a thorough specialist assessment — including urodynamics when clinically indicated — is so important in men presenting with OAB symptoms. It ensures you receive the right treatment, in the right order, with the best chance of success.

Summary

Key Takeaways

OAB is very treatable

From simple lifestyle changes through to Botox and neuromodulation, effective options exist at every level for men with overactive bladder.

Diagnosis is critical in men

Urodynamics can distinguish OAB from prostatic obstruction — a distinction that fundamentally changes the treatment approach and avoids potentially harmful mismanagement.

Specialist assessment matters

Men with urgency and frequency deserve a proper evaluation — not a generic prescription. Our team offers thorough, personalised assessment and a clear treatment pathway.

Overactive bladder in men is common, disruptive, and frequently entangled with prostate symptoms — but with the right assessment, it is very manageable. Our specialists take the time to understand your symptoms, perform the appropriate investigations, and recommend a treatment plan that is both safe and effective for your specific situation.

Whether your symptoms are mild and mainly affecting sleep, or severe and limiting your daily activities, we can help you regain control and improve your quality of life.

Overactive bladder in men requires more than medication alone — it demands a thorough assessment to distinguish OAB from prostatic obstruction. With accurate diagnosis and a personalised treatment plan, the majority of men experience significant and lasting improvement in their symptoms.
