Cancer Clinic

Bladder Cancer

Expert assessment, diagnosis, and treatment of bladder cancer — from early non-muscle-invasive disease to complex muscle-invasive cases.

Overview

About Bladder Cancer

Bladder cancer is one of the most common urological cancers in the UK. The most common type is urothelial carcinoma (transitional cell carcinoma). Early diagnosis is important, as outcomes are generally better when bladder cancer is identified before it invades the muscle layer of the bladder wall.

Risk Factors

Risk Factors

Several factors are associated with an increased risk of bladder cancer. The best established include:

Increasing Age

Smoking

The most important modifiable risk factor.

Occupational Exposure

Certain industrial chemicals.

Male Sex

Previous Pelvic Radiotherapy or Chemotherapy

In some cases.

Chronic Bladder Inflammation or Irritation

In selected cases.

Not all patients with bladder cancer have an identifiable risk factor.

Symptoms

Symptoms of Bladder Cancer

The most common symptom is blood in the urine (haematuria). This may be visible to the naked eye, may come and go, and is often painless. Other symptoms can include:

Common Symptoms

  • Haematuria (blood in the urine)
  • Increased urinary frequency
  • Urgency
  • Dysuria (pain or discomfort when passing urine)
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections
  • Lower abdominal or back pain

Advanced Disease

  • Pelvic pain
  • Flank or back pain
  • Loss of appetite
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • Bone pain or general fatigue in metastatic disease

These symptoms are often caused by non-cancerous conditions such as urinary tract infection, bladder inflammation, or stones. However, any episode of haematuria should be investigated promptly.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis

Initial Investigations

Assessment usually begins with a combination of clinical review and imaging. Investigations may include:

  • Urinalysis
  • Urine culture, where infection is suspected
  • Urine cytology in selected cases
  • Ultrasound or CT urogram
  • Flexible cystoscopy to examine the bladder lining directly

Definitive Diagnosis

If a bladder tumour is suspected, the standard next step is transurethral resection of bladder tumour (TURBT). This allows the tumour to be removed or sampled and provides tissue for histological diagnosis and staging.

NICE recommends that detrusor muscle is obtained at TURBT where appropriate, as this is important for accurate staging.

If muscle-invasive disease is suspected, CT or MRI staging is performed before definitive treatment planning. Urinary biomarkers may be used in specialist settings but should not replace cystoscopy.

Classification

Types of Bladder Cancer

Bladder cancer is broadly divided into two main groups:

Non-Muscle-Invasive (NMIBC)

Ta, T1, and carcinoma in situ (CIS)

Cancer confined to the inner layers of the bladder that has not invaded the detrusor muscle. NMIBC is often treatable, but recurrence is common — careful surveillance is essential.

Muscle-Invasive (MIBC)

T2 and beyond

Cancer that has grown into the bladder muscle. It carries a higher risk of spread and usually requires more intensive treatment with curative intent where possible.

Treatment

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on whether the cancer is non-muscle-invasive or muscle-invasive, as well as tumour grade, stage, overall health, and patient preference.

Non-Muscle-Invasive Bladder Cancer

Management usually begins with TURBT. Following initial resection, some patients are given intravesical therapy — treatment delivered directly into the bladder. Depending on risk category, this may include:

Single Immediate Intravesical Chemotherapy

After first TURBT

Further Intravesical Chemotherapy

Course of treatment

BCG Therapy

For higher-risk disease

Because recurrence is relatively common, follow-up involves regular cystoscopic surveillance, with the schedule determined by low-, intermediate-, or high-risk disease classification.

Muscle-Invasive Bladder Cancer

For suitable patients, curative treatment usually involves one of the following:

Radical Cystectomy with Urinary Diversion

Involves removal of the bladder and reconstruction of urinary drainage, usually with an ileal conduit or, in selected patients, a neobladder. Suitability depends on disease factors and overall fitness.

Bladder-Preserving Treatment

Radiotherapy, often with radiosensitising chemotherapy in selected patients.

Systemic Therapy

Including chemotherapy and, in advanced disease, immunotherapy or other newer systemic treatments where appropriate.

Surveillance

Follow-Up and Surveillance

Bladder cancer requires careful follow-up because recurrence is common, particularly in non-muscle-invasive disease. Surveillance may include:

Flexible Cystoscopy
Urine Cytology
Interval Imaging

NICE follow-up schedules vary by risk group. Low-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer is typically followed with cystoscopy at 3 months and 12 months, while intermediate- and high-risk disease require more intensive surveillance.

Bladder cancer often presents with haematuria, and prompt assessment is essential. Many bladder tumours are non-muscle-invasive and can be managed effectively with endoscopic treatment and intravesical therapy, but they require ongoing surveillance. Muscle-invasive disease needs more intensive treatment and should be managed within a specialist multidisciplinary setting.
