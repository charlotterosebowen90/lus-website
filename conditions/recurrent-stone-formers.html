Understanding the Problem

Overview

Kidney stones are one of the most common urological conditions, and unfortunately they have a high tendency to recur. Up to 50% of patients who form a kidney stone will experience another episode within five years. With each recurrence, the risk of forming yet another stone increases further, creating a frustrating and painful cycle.

For patients who have had two or more stones, or who formed their first stone at a young age, a thorough metabolic evaluation is essential. Without understanding the underlying cause, treatment is purely reactive — addressing each stone as it appears rather than preventing the next one from forming.

Our goal is to break the cycle. Through comprehensive metabolic testing, stone analysis, and personalised dietary and medical strategies, we aim to significantly reduce the risk of future stone episodes and protect long-term kidney health.

Why Stones Come Back

Risk Factors for Recurrence

A number of factors can increase the likelihood that kidney stones will recur. Identifying these risk factors is the first step towards effective prevention.

Lifestyle & Dietary Factors

Low fluid intake — the single most important modifiable risk factor
High sodium diet — increases urinary calcium excretion
Excessive animal protein — lowers urine pH and citrate levels
Obesity — associated with uric acid and calcium oxalate stones

Personal & Family History

Family history of kidney stones — genetic predisposition plays a significant role
Previous stones — each episode increases the risk of the next
Young age at first stone — more lifetime for recurrence

Metabolic Conditions

Primary hyperparathyroidism — causes elevated blood and urinary calcium
Renal tubular acidosis — impairs acid handling by the kidneys
Gout / hyperuricosuria — promotes uric acid stone formation
Cystinuria — inherited condition causing cystine stones

Anatomical Factors

Medullary sponge kidney — dilated collecting ducts predispose to stone formation
PUJ obstruction — impaired drainage promotes crystal aggregation
Horseshoe kidney — abnormal anatomy increases stone risk
The Investigation

Metabolic Evaluation

A comprehensive metabolic workup is the cornerstone of preventing recurrent stones. This involves three key investigations that together give us a detailed picture of why stones are forming.

24-Hour Urine Collection

The single most informative test. We measure:

  • › Total volume — ideally >2.5L/day
  • › Calcium — elevated in hypercalciuria
  • › Oxalate — high levels promote calcium oxalate stones
  • › Citrate — low citrate is a key risk factor
  • › Uric acid — elevated in uric acid stone formers
  • › Sodium — a marker of dietary salt intake
  • › pH — crucial for determining stone type risk

Blood Tests

Essential blood markers include:

  • › Serum calcium — to screen for hyperparathyroidism
  • › Parathyroid hormone (PTH) — if calcium is elevated
  • › Uric acid — elevated in gout and uric acid stones
  • › Renal function (eGFR, creatinine) — assessing kidney health

Stone Analysis

Every stone should be sent for laboratory analysis. This is essential because:

  • › Stone composition directly guides prevention strategy
  • › Mixed stones are common and need tailored approaches
  • › Rare stone types (cystine, struvite) require specialist management

Important: If you pass a stone, always try to collect it and bring it to your appointment for analysis.

Targeted Prevention

Prevention Strategies by Stone Type

The most effective prevention strategy depends on the type of stone you form. Here we outline the evidence-based approaches for each major stone type.

Calcium Oxalate Stones

The most common stone type — approximately 70-80% of all stones

Dietary Measures

  • Increase fluid intake to >2.5L/day
  • Maintain moderate calcium intake — do NOT restrict calcium (this actually increases stone risk)
  • Reduce oxalate-rich foods (spinach, rhubarb, nuts, chocolate, beetroot)
  • Reduce salt intake to <6g/day

Medical Therapies

  • Potassium citrate — raises urinary citrate and pH
  • Thiazide diuretics — reduce urinary calcium excretion in hypercalciuria

Uric Acid Stones

Account for 5-10% of stones — often associated with gout, obesity, and type 2 diabetes

Key Strategies

  • Alkalinise urine — target pH 6.5-7.0 with potassium citrate (uric acid stones dissolve in alkaline urine)
  • Allopurinol — reduces uric acid production if levels remain high
  • Reduce purine-rich foods (red meat, organ meats, shellfish)
  • High fluid intake — aim for >2.5L/day

Good news: Uric acid stones are the only stone type that can potentially be dissolved with medical therapy alone, by alkalinising the urine. This can avoid the need for surgery altogether.

Struvite (Infection) Stones

Caused by urease-producing bacteria — can grow rapidly into staghorn calculi

  • Treat and prevent urinary tract infections — the underlying cause must be addressed
  • Complete stone clearance — residual fragments harbour bacteria and lead to rapid recurrence
  • Long-term low-dose antibiotic prophylaxis may be considered in selected patients

Cystine Stones

Rare inherited condition (cystinuria) — typically presents in childhood or young adulthood

  • Massive hydration — 3L+ per day, including waking at night to drink, to keep urine dilute at all times
  • Urine alkalinisation — target pH >7.0 with potassium citrate
  • Tiopronin or D-penicillamine — cystine-binding drugs that reduce cystine concentration in the urine
  • Reduce salt and animal protein intake
Everyday Changes

Lifestyle & Dietary Advice

Regardless of stone type, certain universal lifestyle measures significantly reduce the risk of recurrence. These are simple changes that can make a big difference.

Hydration

Aim for 2.5-3L of fluid per day. Your urine should be consistently pale straw coloured. Spread intake throughout the day and before bed.

Reduce Salt

Keep sodium intake below 6g/day (about one teaspoon). High salt directly increases calcium excretion in the urine, promoting stone formation.

Balanced Calcium

Maintain a normal calcium intake through food (not supplements). Restricting calcium paradoxically increases oxalate absorption and stone risk.

Limit Animal Protein

Excessive red meat, poultry and fish increase uric acid, lower citrate, and acidify the urine. Aim for moderate portions.

Healthy Weight

Obesity is a significant risk factor for both uric acid and calcium oxalate stones. Maintain a healthy BMI through diet and regular exercise.

Lemon Juice

Freshly squeezed lemon juice is a natural source of citrate, a potent stone inhibitor. Add it to your daily water intake for an easy boost.

Ongoing Care

Long-Term Monitoring

Prevention is a long-term commitment. Regular follow-up allows us to detect new stones early, adjust treatment, and ensure that preventive measures are working effectively.

Regular Imaging

Periodic ultrasound or low-dose CT scanning to monitor for new stone formation or growth of existing residual fragments. The frequency depends on your individual risk profile — typically annually or every 18 months.

Annual Metabolic Bloods

Yearly blood tests to check calcium, uric acid, and renal function. Any changes may indicate a new or evolving metabolic abnormality that needs to be addressed.

Compliance Review

We review your dietary and fluid intake, medication adherence, and repeat 24-hour urine collections if needed to confirm that prevention measures are having the desired effect on urine chemistry.

Medication Adjustment

Medications such as thiazides, potassium citrate, or allopurinol may need dose adjustments over time based on urine chemistry results, side effects, or changes in your overall health.

Key Takeaways

Recurrent stones are common — up to 50% within 5 years — but they are largely preventable with the right approach.

A metabolic evaluation (24-hour urine, bloods, stone analysis) is essential for anyone who has had more than one stone.

Prevention strategies are tailored to stone type — one size does not fit all.

Hydration is the single most important modifiable factor — aim for 2.5-3L/day and pale urine.

Do NOT restrict calcium — this is a common misconception that actually increases stone risk.

Long-term monitoring with regular imaging and metabolic reviews keeps you on track.
