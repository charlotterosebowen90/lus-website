Stone-Related Infections

An infected obstructed kidney is a urological emergency. Early recognition and prompt treatment can be life-saving.

Contact Us
Overview

Stones and Infection: A Dangerous Combination

Kidney and ureteric stones have an intimate and dangerous relationship with urinary tract infection. Stones can harbour bacteria within their crystalline structure, making infections difficult to eradicate with antibiotics alone. Conversely, certain bacteria actively promote stone formation, creating a self-perpetuating cycle of stone growth and recurrent infection.

When a stone obstructs the ureter and infection is present behind the blockage, the kidney becomes a closed, infected space. This condition — known as obstructive pyelonephritis or, when pus accumulates, pyonephrosis — is a true urological emergency. Without rapid drainage, the infection can spill into the bloodstream, causing urosepsis, a life-threatening form of sepsis that can lead to multi-organ failure and death within hours.

Understanding the warning signs and knowing when to seek emergency help is critical. An infected obstructed kidney will not respond to antibiotics alone — the kidney must be drained urgently before definitive stone treatment can be considered.

Types of Infection

Types of Stone-Related Infection

Stone-related infections range from simple urinary tract infections to life-threatening sepsis. Understanding the spectrum helps recognise when urgent intervention is needed.

Moderate

UTI with Coexisting Stone

A urinary tract infection occurring alongside a kidney or ureteric stone. The stone may harbour bacteria, making the infection difficult to clear completely with antibiotics. Requires investigation and stone treatment planning once the acute infection is controlled.

Emergency

Obstructive Pyelonephritis

Infection of the kidney behind an obstructing stone (infected hydronephrosis). The blocked, infected urine cannot drain, and antibiotics alone cannot penetrate effectively. This is a urological emergency requiring urgent decompression of the kidney — typically via a ureteric stent or percutaneous nephrostomy tube.

Critical

Pyonephrosis

Pus accumulating within an obstructed kidney — essentially an abscess of the renal collecting system. Represents the most severe form of obstructive stone infection. The kidney is at risk of irreversible damage or destruction. Requires immediate drainage and IV antibiotics, often with ITU support.

Life-Threatening

Urosepsis

Sepsis originating from the urinary tract, most commonly triggered by an infected obstructed stone. Bacteria and toxins enter the bloodstream, causing a systemic inflammatory response that can lead to septic shock, multi-organ failure, and death. Mortality rates for severe urosepsis can exceed 25–40%. Requires immediate resuscitation, IV antibiotics, and emergency kidney drainage.

Chronic

Struvite (Infection) Stones

Stones formed directly as a result of infection by urease-producing bacteria such as Proteus mirabilis, Klebsiella, and Pseudomonas. These organisms break down urea to produce ammonia, alkalinising the urine and promoting the crystallisation of magnesium ammonium phosphate (struvite). These stones can grow rapidly into large staghorn calculi that fill the entire collecting system. Complete surgical clearance is essential because any residual fragments will harbour bacteria and regrow.

Warning Signs

Symptoms & Warning Signs

The combination of stone symptoms and infection signs should always raise alarm. The key principle is simple: fever plus stone pain equals emergency.

Stone Symptoms

  • ● Severe loin or flank pain (renal colic)
  • ● Pain radiating to the groin
  • ● Nausea and vomiting
  • ● Blood in the urine (haematuria)

Infection Signs

  • ● Fever (temperature ≥ 38°C)
  • ● Rigors (uncontrollable shaking/chills)
  • ● Cloudy or foul-smelling urine
  • ● Burning pain on urination (dysuria)

Sepsis Red Flags

  • ● Rapid heart rate (tachycardia)
  • ● Low blood pressure (hypotension)
  • ● Confusion or altered consciousness
  • ● Rapid or laboured breathing

Emergency Warning

If you have a known kidney stone and develop a fever, attend A&E immediately. An infected obstructed kidney will not resolve with oral antibiotics and requires emergency hospital treatment.

Diagnosis

How We Diagnose Stone-Related Infections

Rapid diagnosis is essential. The following investigations are performed urgently in any patient with suspected infected stone obstruction:

Blood Tests

Full blood count (FBC), C-reactive protein (CRP), lactate level, renal function (creatinine/eGFR), and blood cultures. Elevated white cells, CRP, and lactate indicate severity. Blood cultures identify the causative organism.

CT KUB

Non-contrast CT of the kidneys, ureters, and bladder is the gold standard for identifying stone location, size, and degree of obstruction. It can also reveal gas within the collecting system (emphysematous pyelonephritis) or perinephric abscess.

Ultrasound

Renal ultrasound can rapidly confirm hydronephrosis (swelling of the kidney due to obstruction) and may show echogenic debris within the collecting system suggestive of pyonephrosis. Often used as a bedside first-line test in the emergency setting.

Urine Culture

A midstream urine sample is sent for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity testing. Identifies the infecting organism and guides antibiotic selection. Note: in complete obstruction, the infected urine may be trapped above the stone so a “clean” bladder urine does not exclude infection.

Emergency Treatment

Emergency Management

The immediate priority is to save the patient's life and preserve the kidney. The core principle is: drain the kidney first, treat the stone later. Attempting stone removal during active infection is dangerous and should not be done.

1

Resuscitation & IV Antibiotics

Intravenous fluid resuscitation and broad-spectrum empirical antibiotics are commenced immediately — typically a combination such as piperacillin-tazobactam or a carbapenem. Antibiotics are later refined based on blood and urine culture sensitivities. If the patient is in septic shock, vasopressor support and ITU admission may be required.

2

Emergency Kidney Decompression

The infected, obstructed kidney must be drained urgently. This is achieved by one of two methods:

Ureteric Stent (JJ Stent)

A thin tube inserted via cystoscopy (through the bladder), bypassing the obstructing stone to allow infected urine to drain. Performed under general or local anaesthetic.

Percutaneous Nephrostomy

A tube placed directly through the skin into the kidney under ultrasound or X-ray guidance by an interventional radiologist. Provides direct external drainage of the infected kidney.

3

ITU Care if Septic

Patients with severe sepsis or septic shock require intensive care unit admission for haemodynamic monitoring, vasopressor therapy, and organ support. Multi-disciplinary management involving urologists, intensivists, and microbiologists is essential.

4

Definitive Stone Treatment — Later

Once the infection has fully cleared and the patient has recovered (typically 4–6 weeks later), the stone can be safely treated with definitive surgery. The stent or nephrostomy remains in place to keep the kidney drained during this interval.

Critical Principle

Do NOT attempt stone removal during active infection. The goal of emergency surgery is drainage only. Manipulating the stone in an infected kidney risks worsening sepsis and can be fatal.

Definitive Treatment

Definitive Stone Management

Once the infection has been fully treated and the patient has recovered, definitive stone removal is planned. The choice of procedure depends on stone size, location, composition, and patient factors.

Ureteroscopy (URS)

A thin telescope is passed through the bladder and up the ureter to the stone. The stone is fragmented with a laser and the fragments extracted. Ideal for ureteric stones and smaller kidney stones. No skin incision required.

PCNL (Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy)

A keyhole tract is created through the skin directly into the kidney. A nephroscope is used to fragment and remove large or complex stones, including staghorn calculi. The procedure of choice for large infection stones where complete clearance is essential.

ESWL (Shock Wave Lithotripsy)

External shock waves are focused on the stone to break it into passable fragments. Non-invasive and performed as a day case. Suitable for smaller stones in favourable positions, but less effective for infection stones which tend to be larger and harder.

Complete stone clearance is essential for infection stones. Any residual fragments will harbour bacteria and serve as a nidus for stone regrowth and recurrent infection. Antibiotic prophylaxis is given before and after definitive surgery.

Prevention

Preventing Recurrence

After treatment, a structured prevention plan reduces the risk of further stone-related infections. Our team works with each patient to develop a personalised long-term strategy.

Complete Stone Clearance

Ensuring all stone fragments are removed is the single most important step. Residual fragments serve as a reservoir for bacteria and a seed for new stone growth.

Metabolic Evaluation

A 24-hour urine collection and blood tests assess metabolic risk factors for stone formation, guiding dietary and medical interventions to reduce recurrence.

Long-Term Antibiotics

For patients with recurrent infection stones, long-term low-dose prophylactic antibiotics may be recommended. This is guided by culture sensitivities and reviewed regularly to minimise antibiotic resistance.

Surveillance Imaging

Regular follow-up imaging (ultrasound or low-dose CT) monitors for stone recurrence or residual fragments. Early detection of new stones allows timely treatment before infection develops.

Key Takeaway

Stone-Related Infections Are a Urological Emergency

An infected obstructed kidney can progress to life-threatening urosepsis within hours. If you have a known kidney stone and develop a fever, rigors, or feel systemically unwell, do not wait — present to A&E immediately. The kidney must be drained before the stone can be treated.
