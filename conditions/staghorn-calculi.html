Staghorn Calculi

Large branching kidney stones that fill the renal collecting system, requiring specialist surgical expertise for safe and complete removal.

Overview

What Are Staghorn Calculi?

Staghorn calculi are large, branching kidney stones that occupy the renal pelvis and extend into one or more of the renal calyces, giving them a characteristic shape that resembles a stag's antlers. They are among the most complex stones encountered in urological practice and require specialist management at a centre with the appropriate surgical expertise and facilities.

These stones are most commonly composed of struvite (magnesium ammonium phosphate), also known as infection stones, because they form in the setting of chronic or recurrent urinary tract infections caused by urease-producing bacteria such as Proteus mirabilis and Klebsiella. However, staghorn calculi can also be of mixed composition or, less commonly, composed of uric acid or cystine in patients with underlying metabolic disorders.

Despite their often considerable size, staghorn calculi can be surprisingly asymptomatic for long periods. Many patients are unaware of the stone until it is discovered incidentally on imaging performed for another reason. However, if left untreated, staghorn stones carry a significant risk of progressive renal damage, chronic infection, sepsis, and ultimately loss of kidney function. For this reason, active treatment is almost always recommended.

Why treatment matters

Untreated staghorn calculi are associated with a significant risk of progressive renal deterioration and life-threatening sepsis. Studies suggest that observation alone carries a mortality risk of up to 30% over time. Early referral to a specialist stone centre is strongly recommended.

Types

Types of Staghorn Calculi

Staghorn calculi are classified by their extent within the collecting system and by their mineral composition. Understanding the type of staghorn stone is essential for planning the most effective treatment approach.

By Extent

  • ›
    Complete staghorn — fills the entire renal pelvis and all calyces, creating a cast of the collecting system
  • ›
    Partial staghorn — occupies the renal pelvis and extends into at least one calyx, but does not fill the entire system

By Composition

  • ›
    Struvite (MAP) stones — the most common type, formed in alkaline urine during infections with Proteus, Klebsiella, or Pseudomonas
  • ›
    Uric acid staghorns — associated with metabolic conditions including gout, obesity, and metabolic syndrome
  • ›
    Cystine staghorns — occur in patients with cystinuria, a rare inherited metabolic disorder
Symptoms

Symptoms of Staghorn Calculi

One of the most notable features of staghorn calculi is that they are often surprisingly asymptomatic despite their large size. Because the stone forms gradually within the collecting system and does not typically obstruct the ureter, the classic acute renal colic pain may be absent. Many staghorn stones are discovered incidentally on imaging performed for unrelated reasons.

When symptoms do occur, they may include:

Recurrent Urinary Tract Infections

Repeated UTIs, sometimes with the same organism, are a hallmark sign and may be the initial presentation.

Dull Flank Ache

A persistent, dull pain in the loin or flank rather than the acute, severe colic typically associated with smaller ureteric stones.

Haematuria

Blood in the urine, which may be visible (macroscopic) or detected only on urine testing (microscopic).

Progressive Renal Impairment

Gradual decline in kidney function, often detected on routine blood tests, due to chronic obstruction and infection.

Sepsis Risk

Staghorn calculi associated with infection carry a risk of urosepsis, a potentially life-threatening condition. Symptoms including fever, rigors, confusion, and low blood pressure require emergency medical attention.

Diagnosis

How Are Staghorn Calculi Diagnosed?

Accurate diagnosis and thorough pre-operative assessment are essential for planning the safest and most effective treatment strategy. The following investigations form the cornerstone of the diagnostic workup:

CT KUB (Non-Contrast CT Scan)

The gold standard imaging investigation. CT KUB provides a detailed three-dimensional view of the stone burden, accurately delineating the size, shape, position, and density of the stone. It also identifies the anatomy of the collecting system and any associated complications such as hydronephrosis. This information is critical for surgical planning.

DMSA Renogram

A nuclear medicine scan that assesses the split (differential) renal function — that is, how much each kidney contributes to overall function. This is particularly important for staghorn calculi because it helps determine whether the affected kidney is worth preserving or whether nephrectomy may be the most appropriate option.

Blood Tests

A full panel of blood tests including renal function (creatinine and eGFR), full blood count, inflammatory markers (CRP), electrolytes, calcium, phosphate, uric acid, and parathyroid hormone to assess both kidney function and identify any underlying metabolic causes.

Urine Culture & Analysis

A mid-stream urine sample is sent for culture and sensitivity testing to identify the causative organism and guide appropriate antibiotic therapy before, during, and after any surgical intervention. This is especially important for struvite stones where infection is the driving factor.

Treatment Options

Treatment of Staghorn Calculi

The treatment of staghorn calculi is almost always surgical, as these stones rarely pass spontaneously and carry significant risks if left untreated. The goal is to achieve complete stone clearance wherever possible, as residual fragments — particularly in struvite stones — serve as a nidus for regrowth and recurrent infection. Treatment is tailored to the individual patient based on stone size, composition, renal anatomy, kidney function, and overall fitness.

PCNL — Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy

First-Line Treatment

PCNL is the primary treatment for most staghorn calculi and is recommended by all major international guidelines as the first-line approach. The procedure involves creating a small tract (approximately 1 cm) through the skin of the back directly into the kidney, through which a nephroscope is passed to visualise and fragment the stone using ultrasonic, pneumatic, or laser energy.

For large or complex staghorn calculi, multiple percutaneous tracts or staged procedures may be required to achieve complete clearance. The procedure is performed under general anaesthesia and typically requires a hospital stay of 2–4 days.

Combination Therapy

Multimodal Approach

For complex staghorn stones, a combination of techniques may be employed in a planned, staged fashion to maximise stone-free rates:

  • › PCNL as the primary debulking procedure
  • › Flexible nephroscopy to access and treat stones in difficult-to-reach calyces
  • › ESWL (shockwave lithotripsy) for residual fragments after PCNL
  • › Staged second-look procedures to ensure complete clearance

Flexible Ureteroscopy (RIRS)

Adjunctive Procedure

Retrograde intrarenal surgery using a flexible ureteroscope and holmium laser may be used as an adjunct to PCNL to treat residual fragments in calyces that are difficult to access percutaneously. While not typically used as a standalone treatment for large staghorn stones, RIRS plays a valuable complementary role in achieving stone-free status.

Open / Robotic Surgery

Complex Cases

In highly complex cases — such as very large stones in kidneys with abnormal anatomy, or where multiple percutaneous approaches have been unsuccessful — open or robotic-assisted anatrophic nephrolithotomy may be considered. This involves surgically opening the kidney along its avascular plane to remove the stone intact. While more invasive, it can achieve excellent stone-free rates in carefully selected patients.

Nephrectomy (Kidney Removal)

When the affected kidney has lost most or all of its function as a result of chronic obstruction and infection — confirmed by DMSA renogram showing less than 10–15% differential function — nephrectomy (surgical removal of the kidney) may be the safest and most appropriate option. This eliminates the source of recurrent infection and removes the risk of sepsis from a non-functioning, stone-bearing kidney.

Follow-Up

Follow-Up & Long-Term Care

Achieving stone-free status is the primary surgical goal, but long-term follow-up is equally important to prevent recurrence, monitor kidney function, and detect any residual or new stone formation early.

Stone-Free Rate Assessment

Post-operative imaging (typically a CT KUB at 4–6 weeks) is performed to confirm complete stone clearance. Any significant residual fragments may require a further procedure to reduce the risk of regrowth, particularly for struvite stones.

Metabolic Screening

A comprehensive metabolic evaluation — including 24-hour urine collection for calcium, oxalate, citrate, uric acid, cystine, and pH — is carried out to identify treatable risk factors and guide personalised prevention strategies.

Antibiotic Prophylaxis

For patients with struvite stones, targeted antibiotic therapy is essential. Pre-operative antibiotics are given based on urine culture results, and in some cases long-term low-dose prophylactic antibiotics may be recommended to prevent recurrent infection and stone regrowth.

Long-Term Imaging Surveillance

Regular imaging follow-up with ultrasound or low-dose CT is recommended at intervals of 6–12 months to monitor for stone recurrence. Staghorn stones carry a higher recurrence rate than simple stones, making ongoing surveillance a critical part of long-term care.

Key Takeaway

Staghorn calculi are among the most complex kidney stones encountered in clinical practice. They require expert assessment and treatment at a specialist stone centre with the surgical expertise, equipment, and multidisciplinary support needed to achieve the best possible outcomes.
