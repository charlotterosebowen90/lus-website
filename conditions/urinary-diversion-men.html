Men's Health

Urinary Diversion

Specialist reconstructive surgery to create a new pathway for urine when the bladder has been removed or can no longer function safely.

Overview

What is Urinary Diversion?

When the bladder needs to be removed — most commonly after radical cystectomy for muscle-invasive bladder cancer — or can no longer function safely (for example, due to severe neurogenic bladder or radiation damage), a new way of storing and passing urine must be created surgically. This is called urinary diversion.

The choice of diversion type depends on the underlying condition, cancer stage, urethral health, kidney function, patient fitness, and personal preference. All options are discussed thoroughly before surgery.

In men, neobladder reconstruction is often possible and allows voiding through the urethra without a stoma.

Indications

When Is Urinary Diversion Needed?

After radical cystectomy for muscle-invasive bladder cancer

Severe radiation damage to the bladder

Neurogenic bladder not manageable by other means

Complex or recurrent bladder conditions requiring bladder removal

Types of Diversion

Types of Urinary Diversion

There are three main types of urinary diversion. The most appropriate option for you will depend on your underlying condition, overall health, and personal preferences.

Ileal Conduit (Urostomy)

Most Common

The ileal conduit is the most common and technically straightforward form of urinary diversion. A short segment of the ileum (small bowel) is used to create a conduit. The ureters are connected to one end, and the other end is brought to the skin surface as a stoma. Urine drains continuously into an external stoma bag.

  • Suitable for most patients including older or less fit patients
  • Reliable with well-established long-term outcomes
  • Requires ongoing stoma care — specialist stoma nurses provide training
  • No need for self-catheterisation

Neobladder (Orthotopic Bladder Replacement)

No Stoma Required

A neobladder is a new bladder fashioned from bowel and connected to the urethra. It allows voiding through the urethra in the normal way — without a stoma. This option is more commonly offered to men than women due to better urethral sphincter preservation.

  • Allows voiding without a stoma — through the urethra
  • Not suitable for all patients — depends on cancer location (must not involve the urethra), kidney function, and fitness
  • Good continence rates, though nighttime leakage can occur
  • May require intermittent self-catheterisation if the neobladder doesn't empty completely
  • Requires commitment to long-term follow-up

Continent Cutaneous Diversion

No External Bag

An internal pouch is created from bowel with a channel to the skin. The patient empties the pouch by self-catheterising several times daily. There is no external bag.

  • No external stoma bag required
  • Patient empties by self-catheterising several times daily
  • Less commonly performed in men than ileal conduit or neobladder
  • Suitable when neobladder is not possible but patient wants to avoid a stoma bag
What to Expect

What to Expect

Urinary diversion is a significant surgical procedure. Here is what you can expect at each stage:

1

Pre-operative assessment and stoma siting — full health review, imaging, and marking of the optimal stoma position (for conduit patients) by a specialist stoma nurse.

2

Surgery typically 4-6 hours — often combined with radical cystectomy. Performed under general anaesthesia.

3

Hospital stay 7-14 days — recovery is closely monitored with gradual reintroduction of diet and mobilisation.

4

Stoma nurse support from day one (conduit patients) — training in stoma bag management and skin care.

5

Catheterisation training (neobladder/continent diversion) — teaching you how to empty the reservoir safely and effectively.

6

Gradual return to normal activities — most patients resume normal daily life within 6-12 weeks.

7

Long-term follow-up — regular imaging, blood tests (including B12 and renal function monitoring), and specialist review to ensure ongoing health.

Wellbeing

Emotional Wellbeing & Support

Urinary diversion is a life-changing procedure, and it is completely normal to experience a range of emotions before and after surgery. Our team provides comprehensive support throughout your journey.

Adjusting to Change

Body image concerns, stoma adaptation, and the adjustment period. Many men worry about how a stoma will affect their daily life, work, and relationships.

Psychological Support

Counselling is available through the specialist team. We can arrange support before and after surgery to help you cope with the emotional impact.

Peer Support

The Urostomy Association and Bladder & Bowel Community offer peer support from people who have been through similar experiences.

Your emotional wellbeing matters as much as your physical recovery. Our team includes specialist nurses, counsellors, and support coordinators who are here for you at every stage of your journey — before, during, and long after surgery.

Summary

Key Takeaways

Urinary diversion is a significant procedure, but with specialist surgical care and comprehensive support, most men adapt well and maintain a good quality of life. Where possible, neobladder reconstruction allows voiding without a stoma. The choice of diversion is made together with you, and our reconstructive urology team will support you at every stage.
